## Section 1: Brand Identity
*Last Updated: April 2026 | Version 2.0*

### The One-Sentence Positioning Statement

**itsmomapproved.com earns trust by recommending less.**

Every competitor in this space recommends everything. We don't. We say skip it as readily as we say buy it, and we back both directions with real research. That is what makes the recommendations that do make it through mean something.

### Who We Are

itsmomapproved.com is a research-backed product recommendation site for moms of babies and toddlers aged 0-4. We exist to cut through the noise so Maya can make a confident decision and move on with her life.

We are not a lifestyle blog. We are not an influencer page. We are not a brand partnership platform dressed up as editorial content. We are the friend who has already done the homework — who will tell you "buy it, here's exactly why" and who will also tell you "skip this one, and here's what to get instead."

The second half of that sentence is what separates us. Most affiliate sites only do the first half, because saying "skip it" kills the commission on that product. We say it anyway, because one honest "skip this" earns more trust than ten breathless recommendations — and that trust is what builds the business.

### The Competitive Landscape We're Operating In

Understanding our competition is how we avoid becoming them.

**BabyGearLab** is the gold standard for testing authority. A pediatrician-founded site that buys every product it tests and publishes side-by-side comparison methodology. Trusted by the most research-driven segment of Maya's audience. Limitation: reads like a clinical report. Cold, formatted, impersonal. There is no warmth, no voice, no sense that a real person is talking to her.

**Lucie's List** has authority from its founder's credentials (industrial engineer, certified car seat technician) and a no-ads, reader-supported model. Well-organized and trusted. Limitation: long-form, dense, more textbook than conversation.

**Most other baby affiliate sites** were hit hard by Google's algorithm updates in 2024 and 2025, which specifically penalized sites that reviewed products they clearly never tested. 71% of affiliate review sites saw traffic drops. The ones that survived demonstrated genuine product expertise with visible methodology.

**The gap itsmomapproved.com owns:** The space between clinical authority and paid influencer. Research-backed and genuinely warm. Honest enough to say skip it. Specific enough to earn trust. No other site in this space consistently does both.

### Our Brand Personality

If itsmomapproved.com walked into a party, she wouldn't be the loudest person in the room. She'd be the one that every new mom gravitated toward — because she'd clearly thought about all of this more than anyone else in the room, and she wasn't trying to sell you anything. She's been up at 2am. She's returned three strollers. She's read the one-star reviews so you don't have to. And when you ask her "is the Snoo actually worth it," she doesn't give you a reassuring non-answer. She tells you what she actually thinks.

She is warm but never fluffy. Direct but never cold. Research-backed but never clinical. Relatable but never performative.

### Our Core Values

**Trustworthy.** Every recommendation on this site is earned, not paid for. The willingness to say "skip this one" is the single most important trust signal we have. Protect it on every piece of content we produce.

**Warm.** We write for a mom who is tired, overwhelmed, and doing her absolute best. Every word should feel like it came from someone who genuinely gets it — not someone performing empathy from the outside.

**Research-backed.** We show our methodology, not just our conclusions. Maya needs to see HOW we arrived at a recommendation, not just what we recommend. In 2026, this is both a trust signal and an SEO requirement.

**Specific.** "Good for most babies" means nothing. "Works best for babies over 4 months who are already sleeping in stretches, but struggles with light sleepers under 12 weeks" means something. Specificity is what separates us from every content farm in this space.

**Honest about what not to buy.** This is its own value, because most affiliate sites don't have it. We are not here to move units. We are here to help Maya make the right call — which sometimes means telling her to save her money.

### Our Promise

*When a mom lands on itsmomapproved.com, she will leave with a clear answer, genuine confidence in that answer, and the feeling that someone who actually cares had her back — even when that answer was "don't buy it."*

### Brand Compass

The voice is contextual. Different content types have different jobs.

**For product posts and comparison posts: Write like The Skinny Confidential.** Short sentences. Verdict first. Confidence does the trust-building. No warmup, no over-explaining, no fluff. Maya is close to a decision. Help her close it. The directness is the warmth.

**For pillar pages, safety posts, and emotional content: Write like Dr. Jazmine.** Dr. Jazmine McCoy is a mom psychologist who speaks to moms with genuine warmth and care, without ever being preachy or condescending. For content that touches on how Maya feels — not just what she buys — this is the register. Warm, steady, never clinical, never performative.

**The quality both voices share:** Never preachy. Never condescending. Always specific. Always clear. And both voices show their work — a brief note on how the recommendation was researched earns trust with Maya and signals credibility to Google.

## Section 2: Our Reader — Meet Maya
*Last Updated: April 2026 | Version 2.0*

### Who Maya Is in 2026

Maya is 26-36 years old. She is a millennial or early Gen Z mom in her first or second year of motherhood with one child — a baby or toddler between 3 months and 3 years old. She may be pregnant with her second.

She lives in a mid-to-large city or suburb. She is educated — college degree or higher. Her household income sits around $75,000-$115,000 CAD/USD. Money is not desperate but it is watched. Every purchase over $50 feels like it needs to be justified.

She is more skeptical than the previous generation of new moms. She has watched the influencer industry inflate and deflate. She has read enough "best of" lists that turned out to be SEO spam that she now approaches product recommendations with low-grade suspicion by default. She trusts almost nothing at face value — not the brand, not the influencer, not even the "honest review" site she's never heard of before.

She knows what de-influencing is. She has seen videos of moms telling her NOT to buy things she almost bought, and she trusts those videos more than the ones trying to sell her something. This is important: she is primed to trust a site that tells her to skip things. That is not a liability for itsmomapproved.com. It is the strategy.

### The Millennial-Gen Z Split Within Maya

The core of Maya's audience is millennial (born 1987-1996), but the younger edge is early Gen Z (born 1997-2001). They are not identical.

**Millennial Maya** discovers on Google and Pinterest. She reads full articles. She cross-references. She trusts sites with clear methodology. She responds to research depth and specificity. She is the primary audience for this site's long-form content.

**Gen Z Maya** discovers on TikTok or Instagram Reels first — she saw a 30-second video from a real mom before she ever typed anything into Google. She uses Google and Pinterest to confirm what she already half-believes. She is more value-conscious, more open to secondhand gear, more interested in whether a product is genuinely necessary or just marketed well. She is more likely to be a first-time mom who is also questioning whether she needs all of this stuff in the first place.

Both Mayas land on this site at the same moment: when she's close to a decision and needs someone to close it for her. But the Gen Z Maya arrived here with more skepticism and less patience for over-explanation.

**Write for both. Alienate neither.**

### Her Daily Reality

Maya is tired in a way she didn't fully anticipate. Not just physically — cognitively. She makes hundreds of micro-decisions every day about her child and is quietly terrified of getting the important ones wrong.

She shops in stolen moments. In the pediatrician's waiting room. During nap time with one eye on the monitor. At 11pm after the baby finally settled, lying in bed with her phone six inches from her face. She does not have 45 minutes to read a comprehensive review. She has four minutes and a foggy brain.

She is also starting to question whether she's been sold a version of new parenthood that requires more stuff than it actually does. She has bought things she never used. She has returned things that didn't work the way the review said they would. She is not buying carelessly anymore.

### How Maya Makes Purchase Decisions in 2026

She may have first seen the product on TikTok — a real mom, not a brand, showing it in actual use. She screenshot it, half-forgot about it, and now she's Googling to confirm. She goes to Amazon and reads the one-star reviews first because those are more honest than the five-stars. She asks in her mom group chat. Then she either buys, or gets overwhelmed and closes the tab.

She is deeply influenced by other moms but increasingly skeptical of influencers. She can smell a sponsored post or a paid placement within seconds and it shuts her down completely. What she trusts is specificity and the willingness to say no — a mom who says "I returned this after two weeks because the motor is too loud for a light sleeper" is more convincing than ten perfect testimonials.

She is also aware of the affiliate model. She knows that "this post may contain affiliate links" usually means the site has a financial incentive to tell her everything is great. She's not naive. The only thing that overcomes this suspicion is visible evidence of honesty — a negative recommendation, a real caveat, a specific reason not to buy something.

### Her Emotional State When She Lands on Our Site

She arrived with a specific question and she is hoping someone will give her a clear, honest answer. She is slightly overwhelmed before she even clicks. If the verdict is buried or qualified beyond use, she will leave in under 30 seconds.

She wants to feel like she found a friend who has done more homework than she has and is going to tell her straight — including straight about what not to buy.

### What Maya Values (In Order)

**Honesty about what not to buy.** This is number one in 2026. She trusts us more when we tell her to skip something — or tell her the real downsides — than when we recommend everything. A post that has a genuine "we wouldn't buy this" section converts better than one that doesn't.

**Safety.** Is it actually safe, or does it just sound safe? Has anyone flagged it for harmful chemicals, recalls, or safety concerns? Give her the real answer.

**Budget reality.** Is there a version that does the same thing for less? Or is this one genuinely worth the price jump? She needs the honest call — not hedge language.

**Longevity.** She has been burned by products that worked beautifully at 4 months and ended up on Facebook Marketplace by spring. She wants to know if this is a phase product or a long-haul one.

**How you researched it.** She wants to know your methodology in one or two sentences. Not a dissertation — just enough to know you didn't just Google it and write it up. This is what separates a recommendation she can trust from content she ignores.

**Easy to act on.** Clear link. Clear verdict. No endless scrolling. She is reading one-handed.

### The Maya Test

Before publishing anything, run it through these four questions:

- Does this give Maya a clear answer within the first three sentences?
- Would Maya feel respected or talked down to by this content?
- Is there anything in here that would make Maya think "this site is just trying to sell me something"?
- Would Maya send this to her mom group chat?

*If the answer to question 4 is yes, publish it. If not, revise until it is.*

## Section 3: Voice and Tone
*Last Updated: April 2026 | Version 2.0*

### The Voice in One Paragraph

itsmomapproved.com sounds like a smart, warm, slightly tired mom who has done real research and wants to get to the point. She is not performing enthusiasm. She is not hedging to sound balanced. She is not padding sentences to sound thorough. She says what she means, backs it up briefly with how she knows, and trusts the reader to be an adult. She is the friend who texts you "get the Hatch, skip the Marpac, here's why — and yes, I looked this up before I told you" and you know she already did the work.

The thing that makes this voice distinctive: she will tell you not to buy something just as readily as she'll tell you to buy it. And she'll tell you how she figured that out.

### Voice Pillars

**Lead with the answer.** Maya arrived with a question. Answer it first. Everything else — context, research, caveats — comes after. Never bury the verdict.

**Show your methodology briefly.** Not a dissertation. One to two sentences: "We analyzed 3,000 Amazon reviews, cross-referenced two pediatric safety databases, and compared pricing across six retailers." That one sentence is what separates a recommendation Maya trusts from content she ignores. Google rewards it too.

**Be specific, not general.** "Good for most babies" says nothing. "Works best for babies over 4 months who are already sleeping in stretches, but consistently struggles with light sleepers under 12 weeks based on the 1-star review patterns" says something.

**Honest about what not to buy.** Every piece of content where we skip or downgrade a product should say so clearly and immediately redirect Maya to something better. This is not a liability — it is the brand.

**Earn every word.** No filler. No throat-clearing. No sentences that exist only to transition to the next sentence. If a sentence does not earn its place it gets cut.

**Acknowledge her reality.** She is tired, budget-conscious, slightly skeptical, and short on time. Write for her actual life — not an idealized version of new motherhood.

### Voice in Action — Before and After

**Example 1: Opening a comparison post**

Before — Do not write this:
*"Are you trying to decide between the Hatch Rest and the Hatch Rest+? You've come to the right place! In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through everything you need to know."*

Why it's wrong: It wastes time and screams "content machine."

After — This is the standard:
*"Get the Hatch Rest. Unless you specifically want portability or a built-in audio monitor — in that case, the $20 upgrade to the Rest+ is worth it. Here's how we landed there."*

Why it works: Verdict in two sentences. Caveat is specific and conditional. The last line signals that methodology is coming.

**Example 2: Negative recommendation**

Before — Do not write this:
*"While this product has received mixed reviews, it may still work well for some families depending on individual needs and preferences."*

Why it's wrong: A non-answer. Helps nobody and signals that the site won't tell her anything real.

After — This is the standard:
*"We're not recommending the DockATot. The recall history and suffocation risk warnings from the AAP are disqualifying, regardless of how many Instagram moms you've seen use it. Here's what to get for safe lounging instead."*

Why it works: Takes a real position. Cites a real reason. Immediately redirects.

**Example 3: Showing methodology**

Before — Do not write this:
*"After extensive research, we found that the Ergobaby Omni 360 is the top-rated carrier for newborns."*

Why it's wrong: "Extensive research" means nothing. It's a phrase that signals the opposite of research.

After — This is the standard:
*"We reviewed 4,700 Amazon reviews, looked at a certified babywearing educator's breakdown of hip development positioning, and compared hip-healthy certifications across six carriers. The Ergobaby Omni 360 came out ahead — here's why, and where it falls short."*

Why it works: Tells Maya exactly how you know what you know. Builds instant credibility. Signals honesty because you also told her where it falls short.

**Example 4: Talking about price**

Before — Do not write this:
*"While this product is on the pricier side, many parents find it well worth the investment."*

Why it's wrong: Vague and passive.

After — This is the standard:
*"It's $189. Here's the honest framework: if your baby fights sleep and you're running on 3-hour nights, this pays for itself in sanity inside two weeks. If your baby sleeps reasonably well already, the $40 version does 80% of the same job — save the money."*

Why it works: Names the price. Gives Maya a specific decision framework for her actual situation.

### Hard Rules — Non-Negotiable

**NEVER use em dashes (—) anywhere. Not in the post, not in the title, not in the headers.** The voice is conversational, like a text from a trusted friend. Em dashes belong in editorial writing, not in this register. Restructure the sentence instead. In titles, use colons: "Hatch Rest vs Hatch Rest+: Which One Is Worth It?" not "Hatch Rest vs Hatch Rest+ — Which One Is Worth It?"

**No "actually," "finally," or "it's worth noting" — anywhere, including titles and headers.** These read as condescending. They imply Maya hadn't thought of something obvious. We assume she's smart.

**No hedge stacking.** One caveat per point is honest. Two or three on the same point is cowardice. Pick the most important qualification and state it once, cleanly.

**No passive voice recommendations.** "This product is loved by many moms" is not a recommendation. "We recommend this" or "skip this one" are recommendations. Own the verdict.

**No question openers.** "Looking for the best baby monitor?" is not an opener — she knows she's looking for one, she typed it. Start with the answer.

**No listicle padding.** If only four of the eleven products are genuinely worth recommending, the post has four products.

**Always name the price.** Never "affordable," "budget-friendly," or "an investment." Name the actual dollar amount.

**Always include a budget alternative** unless the cheaper version presents a genuine safety concern or is significantly inferior.

**Always show methodology.** One to two sentences explaining how the recommendation was researched. This is now both a brand standard and a Google ranking requirement.

### Tone Adjustments by Content Type

**Comparison posts:** Skinny Confidential register. Verdict in the first two sentences. Methodology note immediately after. Personality in word choice and one or two lines with edge. 1,000-1,800 words. Maya is close to a decision — help her close it.

**Best-of / pillar pages:** Dr. Jazmine register. Open by acknowledging what Maya is trying to solve, then move into the answer with methodology. This is the content she bookmarks and sends to her group chat. 1,800-2,400 words. Do not go longer unless the topic genuinely requires it — usefulness density matters more than length.

**"Skip This" posts:** Skinny Confidential register with extra directness. These are negative recommendation posts. State what the product is, why it doesn't make the cut, what the specific concern is, and exactly what to get instead. These are among the highest trust-building posts the site can publish — treat them as a feature, not a warning label.

**Safety posts:** Dr. Jazmine register, moving faster to the facts. Warm and steady, never alarmist. State the concern, name who it applies to, give a clear action. Maya is already anxious when she lands on a safety post — don't add to it and don't waste her time.

**Registry Reality posts:** Dr. Jazmine register. These are posts that answer "what do I actually need vs what do I think I need." They are the highest-value content for a first-time pregnant Maya. Honest, specific, includes a "don't bother" section. These build email list subscribers faster than any other content type.

**Email newsletter:** The closest thing to a real text from a friend. Short sentences, personal opener, one clear recommendation per issue. Dr. Jazmine warmth with Skinny Confidential efficiency. End with a direct question to Maya or a single action she can take.

**Pinterest captions:** Pure Skinny Confidential. Lead with the most specific, searchable version of the verdict. One concrete detail. One CTA. Done. No em dashes.

**Social media:** Skinny Confidential voice, Dr. Jazmine warmth. Reads like Maya's most trusted friend posted it, not a brand account.

### The Voice Test

Before finalizing any piece of content, read it out loud. Then ask:

- Does this sound like a real person or a content machine?
- Would a tired mom at 11pm read past the first two sentences?
- Is there a single sentence that could be cut without losing anything?
- Does it show — not just claim — that this recommendation was researched?
- Does it earn Maya's trust or just ask for it?

*If any answer flags something, fix it before publishing.*

## Section 4: Content Standards and Hard Rules
*Last Updated: April 2026 | Version 2.0*

### Why This Section Exists

These are the rules that protect the brand from sounding like every other affiliate site Maya has already learned to ignore. Every one of them exists because a site that breaks it is a site she will trust less. Read this section before every content session.

### The Non-Negotiable Rules

**Rule 1: The verdict comes first.**
Every product post, every email recommendation, every Pinterest caption leads with the conclusion. Maya does not scroll to find out what we think. She finds out in the first two sentences or she leaves.

**Rule 2: Every recommendation requires visible evidence.**
"Moms love this" is not evidence. "4.7 stars across 28,000 Amazon reviews with consistent praise for durability past 18 months, and no flagged recalls" is evidence. Every recommendation cites at least one concrete data point.

**Rule 3: Show the methodology.**
One to two sentences on how the recommendation was researched — what sources were used, how many reviews were analyzed, whether expert sources were consulted, whether products were personally tested. This is not optional. It is required on every post.

**Rule 4: Every post must include all four content pillars.**
No post goes live without a safety note, a budget note, a longevity note, and a clear affiliate link. These four are non-negotiable regardless of content type.

**Rule 5: Never recommend a product below our minimum standard.**
Minimum threshold: 4.2 stars with at least 500 reviews, or equivalent credibility from a verified expert source. No exceptions.

**Rule 6: Always name the price.**
Every recommendation includes the actual dollar amount. No exceptions.

**Rule 7: Every post includes a budget alternative.**
Unless the cheaper version presents a genuine safety concern or is significantly inferior in a way Maya would care about, every post offers a lower-cost option.

**Rule 8: Negative recommendations are a feature, not a warning label.**
When research points away from a product, say so clearly, give the reason, and immediately redirect Maya to something better. The willingness to say "skip this" is the single most important trust signal on the site. A "We Wouldn't Buy" or "Skip This" section in a post is valuable — not something to avoid.

**Rule 9: Every post must include an FTC disclosure.**
Legal requirement (US and Canada). Goes at the very top of every post, before any content. Use this exact language:

> Heads up: this post contains affiliate links. If you buy through our links, we may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. As an Amazon Associate, we earn from qualifying purchases. We research every product we recommend. We only share what we'd tell a friend.

The "As an Amazon Associate" sentence is required word-for-word by Amazon's program terms. It cannot be paraphrased.

**Rule 10: Every cluster post links back to its pillar page.**
Internal linking builds topic authority with Google. Every comparison post and cluster page includes at least one link back to its pillar page. Confirm the pillar before drafting.

**Rule 11: Every post generates three Pinterest pins.**
Pinterest is the primary traffic channel in year one. Three pins with distinct headlines per post is the minimum. Required deliverable, not an afterthought.

**Rule 12: Content length must match the job.**
Comparison posts: 1,000-1,800 words. Pillar pages: 1,800-2,400 words. Longer is not better. Usefulness density is the measure. If a post can give Maya everything she needs in 900 words, 900 words is the right length.

### The Pre-Publish Checklist

- Does the post lead with a clear verdict in the first two sentences?
- Does every recommendation include a specific data point as evidence?
- Is there a methodology note (1-2 sentences on how this was researched)?
- Are all four content pillars present: safety, budget, longevity, affiliate link?
- Is the price named as an actual number?
- Is there a budget alternative?
- Has the hedge spiral been checked and removed?
- Does the post end with something useful — a practical tip, a direct action, or a warm closing line — not a summary?
- Does any sentence or header contain an em dash, a question opener, stacked hedges, passive voice recommendation, or the words "actually" / "finally"?
- Does any sentence sound generated rather than written?
- Would Maya send this to her mom group chat?
- Is the FTC disclosure at the very top of the post?
- Does the post link back to its pillar page?
- Has the voice register been checked — Skinny Confidential for product/comparison posts, Dr. Jazmine for pillar/safety/emotional?
- Are three Pinterest pin captions written and ready?
- Is the post within the correct length range for its content type?

*Every answer must be yes before publishing.*

### Failure Mode Warnings

**Failure Mode 1: The Confident Opener That Says Nothing**
What it looks like: *"Finding the right baby monitor can feel overwhelming with so many options on the market today."*
The fix: Delete it. Start with the answer.

**Failure Mode 2: The Hedge Spiral**
What it looks like: *"While this product generally works well for most babies, results may vary depending on your individual child's temperament, sleeping habits, and developmental stage."*
The fix: One caveat, stated once, cleanly. Cut the rest.

**Failure Mode 3: The AI Tell**
What it looks like: *"In conclusion, the [product] is a fantastic choice for new moms looking for a reliable, high-quality option."*
The fix: End with something useful. A final specific detail, a practical tip, a warm direct line.

**Failure Mode 4: The Fake Research Signal**
What it looks like: *"After extensive research, we found..."* or *"We thoroughly tested..."* without a single specific detail about what the research was.
The fix: Name the actual sources. Number of reviews analyzed. Expert credentials cited. Products personally tested or curated. Vague claims of research are worse than no claim at all — Maya sees through them immediately.

**Failure Mode 5: The Vague Safety Section**
What it looks like: *"As always, safety is our top priority. Please follow all manufacturer guidelines and consult your pediatrician."*
The fix: Be specific every single time. Name the material. Name who it applies to. Give a clear action.

**Failure Mode 6: Recommending Everything**
What it looks like: A "best of" post where everything is good and nothing is skipped.
The fix: Every post should have at least one thing it explicitly does not recommend, with a reason. If every product made the cut, the research wasn't honest enough.

**Failure Mode 7: Writing for Google Instead of Maya**
What it looks like: Keyword stuffing, repetition that reads as SEO-driven, lists of synonyms.
The fix: Write for Maya first. Use the primary keyword naturally in the title, first paragraph, and one subheading. After that, write like a person.

## Section 5: Business Logic
*Last Updated: April 2026 | Version 2.0*

### Content Architecture — Topic Clusters

itsmomapproved.com is built around six content pillars: Sleep, Feeding, Carriers and Strollers, Play and Development, Just for Mama, and Safety. Every post belongs to one of these six pillars. Each pillar has one authoritative pillar page supported by cluster pages that link back to it.

**The six pillar pages:**
- Sleep: Best White Noise Machines for Babies
- Feeding: Best Baby Bottles for Breastfed Babies
- Carriers and Strollers: Best Baby Carriers for Newborns
- Play and Development: Best Play Mats for Babies
- Just for Mama: Best Postpartum Recovery Essentials
- Safety: Best Baby Monitors

Every post produced belongs to one of these pillars. Every comparison and cluster post links back to its pillar page.

### Content Type Definitions

**Comparison posts (30% of output):** "X vs Y: Which One Is Worth It?" High-intent, close-to-purchase traffic. Highest commercial value. Target these first for each pillar. Word count: 1,000-1,800.

**Best-of / pillar pages (30% of output):** "Best [Category] for [Qualifier]." Broad search traffic, multiple recommendations at different price points. Word count: 1,800-2,400.

**"Skip This" posts (10% of output):** Explicit negative recommendation posts. "Why We Don't Recommend [Product]" or "Baby Products Worth Skipping." Highest trust-building content on the site. Underused by every competitor. These make Maya trust every positive recommendation more. Word count: 800-1,200.

**Registry Reality posts (10% of output):** "What You Actually Need on Your Baby Registry (And What's a Waste of Money)." Pillar-level content that earns email signups faster than any other format. High-value for pregnant Maya. Word count: 1,800-2,400.

**Problem-solution posts (15% of output):** "My Baby Won't Sleep Without Being Held: What Worked and What Didn't." Connects a pain point to a product recommendation. Medium commercial value, high emotional resonance.

**Safety and research posts (5% of output):** Recall coverage, safety standards explainers, "what the research actually says" posts. Builds domain authority and drives email signups. Not primarily commercial.

### How itsmomapproved.com Makes Money

The business operates on an affiliate marketing model. When Maya clicks a link and makes a purchase, itsmomapproved.com earns a commission. Maya pays nothing extra.

**Affiliate Program Priority Hierarchy:**

Tier 1 — Prioritize first: ShareASale network brands (8-15% commission, 30-60 day cookies). Direct brand affiliate programs — always check brand website footers first, sometimes 15-20%. Rakuten Advertising.

Tier 2 — Use strategically: Amazon Associates (1-4% commission, 24-hour cookie). Use for products where no higher-tier affiliate program exists. The short cookie window and low commissions make this a second-choice, not a default.

Tier 3 — Use sparingly: CJ Affiliate and Impact Radius.

**Cookie window rule:** When two affiliate options exist for the same product, choose the longer cookie window unless the commission rate difference is significant enough to offset it.

### Tech Stack

**CMS:** Sanity — manages all content and stores affiliate product data (links, images, descriptions, commission tiers).

**Analytics:** Supabase (via Lovable Cloud) — logs click events independently of affiliate network reporting, giving visibility into which products Maya actually clicks on and which posts drive the most affiliate activity.

**Affiliate network:** Handles conversion and commission tracking.

**Publishing platform:** Lovable (React SPA). Prerendering layer required for full SEO indexing — ensure this is in place before scaling content.

### The Email List Is the Business

The email list is the only audience asset itsmomapproved.com owns outright. Every piece of content has one secondary goal beyond the affiliate commission: capture Maya's email address.

- 100 subscribers: Founding members. Reply personally where possible.
- 500 subscribers: Newsletter sponsorship pitches to small brands become realistic.
- 2,000 subscribers: The list is a meaningful asset for brand partnership conversations.
- 5,000 subscribers: Newsletter sponsorship revenue becomes a reliable secondary income stream.

Registry Reality posts and Safety posts are the highest email signup drivers. Prioritize these when building toward list milestones.

### Pinterest Is the Primary Traffic Channel

Every published post generates a minimum of three pins with distinct headlines and visuals. Pin headlines are specific, searchable, and lead with the clearest version of the verdict or the most useful specific detail.

Pinterest's user base in the parent/baby category researches products before buying — 64% of parents who use Pinterest monthly research a product online before purchasing. This is not a casual social network for this audience. It is a discovery and purchase intent engine. Treat it accordingly.

### Seasonal Content Calendar

**January-February:** New year. Storage solutions, sleep training gear, developmental toys for new milestones.
